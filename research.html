Research

Asteroseismology: a brief overview

I am an FWO senior postdoctoral research fellow based at the Institute of Astronomy at the KU Leuven in Belgium. My research interests include asteroseismology of high- and intermediate-mass stars, including beta Cephei, SPB, delta Scuti, roAp and gamma Doradus stars. Asteroseismology uses stellar oscillation frequencies to probe stellar structure and evolution, with the ultimate goal being to improve theoretical models for a diverse range of stars. The quality of space observations, such as from the CoRoT, Kepler/K2 and TESS space telescopes, provide an excellent opportunity to study physics that is not currently well understood for massive stars.

My current research grant, TESSERACT, is using asteroseismology to determine the (convective core) masses, ages and interior rotation and mixing profiles of massive stars. These properties are not accurately known for massive stars, because models contain large uncertainties. Massive stars pulsate in gravity (g) and pressure (p) mode oscillations which probe their deep stellar interiors and radiative envelopes, thus provide excellent insight of the physics at work within stellar interiors. The ongoing TESS mission is targeting thousands of massive stars, which the TESSERACT project is exploiting using asteroseismology combined with high-resolution spectroscopy from ESO and Mercator.

What are gravity waves?

I am also interested in the search for and interpretation of the signatures of internal gravity waves in massive stars. In the past, I performed this research under the framework of Prof. Conny Aerts' ERC advanced grant: MAMSIE (Mixing and Angular Momentum tranSport of massIvE stars). The MAMSIE team consists of academics, post-docs and PhD students from within the KU Leuven, and has strong links to the research team led by Professor Tamara Rogers at Newcastle University in the UK. The goal of MAMSIE is to combine these observations with 3D hydrodynamical simulations to improve our understanding of stellar structure and evolution for massive stars. More specifically, constraints from seismic observables of gravity waves allow prescriptions for angular momentum transport caused by internal gravity waves and mixing by turbulent entrainment to be formulated. Any star with a convective core is expected to excite gravity waves, and there are no restrictions on whether a star is a fast or slow rotator, the strength of its magnetic field or the presence of a stellar wind.
